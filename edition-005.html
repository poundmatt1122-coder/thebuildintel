Executive Summary

Two deadlines land in the same six-month window. One threatens the $350 billion federal highway funding backbone of western US civil construction. The other threatens the water supply that makes large-scale development possible across the desert states. Either alone would be a significant market disruption. Together they represent the most consequential policy moment for western US construction since IIJA passed in 2021.

The base case is a short-term Continuing Resolution on IIJA and a lower-basin bridge agreement on the Colorado River — creating a period of uncertainty in Q4 2026 and Q1 2027 followed by recovery. The Desert Southwest faces the most acute combined exposure. A Phoenix contractor bidding a data center or semiconductor project in 2027 could face a project that loses its federal infrastructure reimbursement AND its water permit in the same 90-day window.

Three actions before year-end 2026

1Bid aggressively on every IIJA-funded DOT letting before September 30. The authorization is active today — use it before Congressional uncertainty slows new lettings regardless of outcome. FHWA ↗
2Verify water availability certificates for every Desert Southwest project in your 2027 pipeline. If you are bidding data center, semiconductor, or large industrial work in Phoenix, Las Vegas, or Tucson — check ADWR certificate availability before pricing. ADWR ↗
3Engage your state contractor associations now. ARTBA, AGC, and AASHTO are actively advocating for reauthorization. Your regional project data matters in this process. ARTBA ↗
Part 1 — The IIJA Funding Cliff

The BUILD America 250 Act passed the House T&I Committee 62-2 on May 22, 2026. Full reauthorization before September 30 is unlikely. A Continuing Resolution is the base case — but even a CR creates real disruption for new project lettings. House T&I ↗

The IIJA has been the single most important demand driver for western US civil construction since 2021. California alone received an estimated $41.9 billion in formula funding over FY2022–2026. Caltrans ↗ Every western state DOT has built its capital program around this funding stream. The question is not whether projects already under construction continue — they do, under any scenario. The question is whether new contracts can be awarded after September 30.

Congressional reauthorization faces three compounding headwinds: partisan disagreement on funding levels, Highway Trust Fund solvency challenges, and an August recess that compresses the legislative calendar to weeks not months. Senate EPW has held hearings but no markup is scheduled. Senate EPW ↗ ARTBA's "Build America Now" campaign is actively lobbying for a five-year reauthorization at 20% above current IIJA levels. ARTBA ↗ AASHTO has warned a lapse would delay $48 billion in projects nationally, with western states accounting for approximately 38% of those delays. AASHTO ↗

"Historical precedent from 2012 and 2015 shows that extended authorization lapses reduced state DOT letting by an average of 15–20% during the lapse period. Western states with the largest IIJA-funded Q4 letting schedules face the sharpest near-term exposure." FHWA ↗

Part 2 — The Colorado River Deadline

Lake Mead is at approximately 1,045 feet — Tier 1 shortage conditions in effect. Bureau of Reclamation's draft EIS proposes cuts of 760,000 acre-feet to Arizona, 440,000 to California, and 50,000 to Nevada. The Final EIS is expected this summer. Seven-state consensus has not been reached. USBR ↗

The Colorado River has been the lifeblood of western development for a century. The proposed post-2026 operating rules represent the most significant reallocation of western water rights in decades. For the construction industry the stakes are direct — water availability is no longer just an environmental consideration. In Phoenix, Las Vegas, Tucson, and parts of Southern California it is a gating permit requirement for large construction projects. A data center, semiconductor fab, or large industrial facility that cannot demonstrate adequate water supply cannot get its construction permit approved. ADWR ↗

"A 100MW hyperscale data center requires approximately 1–3 million gallons of water per day for cooling. In a market where Arizona faces a proposed 760,000 acre-foot Colorado River reduction, the water availability certificate that enables project approval is not guaranteed." CAP Arizona ↗

Lake Mead and Lake Powell — Current Status

Lake Mead — ~1,045 feet elevation Tier 1 Shortage USBR Lower CO ↗

Critically low. Tier 1 shortage conditions in effect. Forecasts through 2027 project continued pressure given current inflow conditions. No meaningful recovery expected without above-average snowpack. Each additional Tier level triggers mandatory additional cuts to Lower Basin allocations.

Lake Powell — Upper Basin reservoir stressed USBR Glen Canyon ↗

Upper Basin storage stressed. Glen Canyon Dam power generation affected at low elevations. Colorado, Utah, Wyoming, and New Mexico face increasing pressure to reduce Upper Basin consumption to protect minimum pool levels.

Lower Basin bridge proposal — AZ, CA, NV joint submission USBR Post-2026 ↗

Lower Basin states submitted a joint bridge proposal for 2026–2028 operating rules. No full seven-state consensus reached as of late June 2026. BOR Final EIS expected summer 2026 — watch for publication date announcement as it triggers the formal comment and decision timeline.

Part 3 — The Combined Impact

The Desert Southwest is the only western market facing acute exposure to both deadlines simultaneously. Arizona sits at the intersection of the largest IIJA-funded DOT letting schedule in the desert region AND the largest proposed Colorado River water cut of any state.

The combined scenario analysis reveals a clear geographic risk gradient. The Desert Southwest — particularly Arizona — carries compounding exposure no other western market faces. California is highly IIJA-dependent but has more water supply flexibility through MWD. MWD ↗ Washington and Oregon face IIJA risk but are largely insulated from Colorado River water concerns. Wyoming and Idaho carry less IIJA exposure and no Colorado River dependency.

Week in Review — Top 3 Takeaways
1

The most likely IIJA outcome is a Continuing Resolution — not a lapse, not full reauthorization. But a CR is not a green light. State DOTs hesitate to let large multi-year contracts when their authorization expires in weeks. Civil and highway contractors should bid every IIJA-funded DOT letting before September 30. Check your state DOT calendar today. An estimated $5.4 billion in western state Q4 2026 lettings are IIJA-funded and on the clock.

FHWA ↗ ARTBA ↗ AASHTO ↗ Funding Landscape ↗
2

Arizona contractors and suppliers face the most acute combined exposure of any western market. A proposed −760,000 acre-foot Colorado River reduction directly constrains ADWR water availability certificates — the gating permit for large construction projects in Phoenix, Tucson, Scottsdale, Mesa, Chandler, and Gilbert. Any contractor bidding a data center, semiconductor facility, or large industrial project in the Phoenix market for 2027 delivery must verify water certificate availability before pricing the work. This is now pre-bid due diligence, not an environmental footnote.

ADWR ↗ CAP Arizona ↗ USBR ↗ CleanTechnica ↗
3

The Pacific Northwest is the most insulated western market from both deadlines. WSDOT and ODOT face IIJA reauthorization risk like every other western DOT. But the PNW has no Colorado River exposure and water is not a construction permit gating factor. If your business has geographic flexibility and you are evaluating where to allocate bidding resources in H2 2026 and early 2027 — the Pacific Northwest and Mountain West carry meaningfully lower combined risk than the Desert Southwest and California.
