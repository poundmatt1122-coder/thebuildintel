From the desk of Matt

Fuel pricing relief is here — but is it here to stay? While I don’t have a crystal ball, I am making sure I do as much research as possible to avoid falling asleep at the wheel and getting caught off guard by rising fuel costs in the coming months as crude oil stabilizes.

What we are seeing right now is real relief — California diesel down nearly 77 cents in a single month, and asphalt binder down $108 per ton. For paving contractors specifically, this is the best cost environment we have tracked since launching this newsletter. But the structural forces that drove prices to their peak have not gone away. The LCFS regulatory update that took effect July 1 and the continued drawdown of refinery capacity from the Valero Benicia closure are both building through summer. This is a window to act, not a signal that costs have normalized.

I hope this research helps as you plan your own bids and operations. As always, I look forward to hearing from readers on what topics we should dig into next — your feedback makes this better every week.

Diesel Deep Dive — Relief Is Real But Temporary

California diesel fell $0.770/gal in a single month. Asphalt binder fell $108.00/ton — a 19.1% single-month drop. Two of the largest input costs for paving contractors declined simultaneously in July. This is the most significant petroleum input cost relief we have tracked since launching this newsletter. The window to act is open. It may not stay open through August. AAA ↗ Caltrans ↗

The relief is welcome and real. California diesel peaked at $7.747/gallon in April, spent weeks above $7.00, and has now pulled back to $6.479. That $1.27 decline from the peak translates directly into lower equipment operating costs, lower fuel surcharges on material deliveries, and improved bid economics on open projects. For a paving contractor running five pieces of equipment 10 hours a day, this drop is thousands of dollars per week back in the margin column. AAA California ↗

But the structural picture has not improved. The forces that drove California diesel above $7.00 have not gone away — and three new headwinds are building simultaneously that make this relief window shorter than it looks. California fuel analysis ↗

▼ Major Move — Asphalt Binder

Combined with the $0.770/gal diesel decline, paving contractors are seeing their two largest input costs drop simultaneously for the first time since this newsletter launched. This window is real — and it may not last through August. Caltrans index ↗

Three Headwinds That Could Push Prices Back Above $7.00

Headwind 1 — Regulatory

LCFS update effective July 1, 2026

California's Low Carbon Fuel Standard update took effect July 1 — the same week diesel prices were declining. LCFS compliance costs could add up to $0.65/gallon as the new requirements phase in through Q3 and Q4 2026. This cost is structural — it does not reverse with crude oil prices. Source ↗

Potential impact: +$0.65/gal by Q4 2026

Headwind 2 — Supply

Valero Benicia full impact building through August

The Valero Benicia closure removed 145,000 barrels per day of California refining capacity. USC and UC Berkeley analysis projects this closure adds an estimated $1.21/gallon by August 2026 as inventories draw down and the state increases reliance on imports from Asia. The impact has not yet fully materialized. Energy News Beat ↗

Potential impact: +$1.21/gal by August 2026

Headwind 3 — Geopolitical

Strait of Hormuz crude oil pressure

Brent crude rose from $71/barrel in February to $117/barrel in April due to Strait of Hormuz supply disruption. EIA forecasts diesel at $3.40/gallon nationally in 2026 — but California pays a $1.20+ premium above national average due to CARB regulations, unique fuel blend requirements, and import dependence. Any renewed crude price spike hits California harder than any other state. EIA STEO ↗

Floor risk: California premium vs national persists regardless of crude

TSMC Arizona — The Biggest Construction Story in the Western US

Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company just accelerated its Arizona timeline. Fab 2 equipment installation is moving into Q3 2026. Fab 3 is already under construction. Permits are advancing for a fourth fab and an advanced packaging facility. The Phoenix construction market is absorbing a demand surge with no parallel in the western US. AZ Tech Council ↗

TSMC is the world's largest dedicated semiconductor foundry — it manufactures chips designed by Apple, NVIDIA, AMD, and Qualcomm. Its Arizona investment of $165 billion represents the largest single foreign direct investment in US manufacturing history. The construction demand it creates is not abstract — every fab requires hundreds of thousands of cubic yards of concrete, enormous structural steel frameworks, ultra-pure water systems, and specialized cleanroom construction labor that must be sourced from across the entire western US. Colorado River Update — Final EIS Due Late July

The Bureau of Reclamation announced emergency protective operations this week — releasing 660,000 to 1 million acre-feet from Flaming Gorge Reservoir and reducing Powell-to-Mead releases by 1.48 million acre-feet through September. The Final EIS that sets post-2026 water rules is expected late July. USBR ↗

This is a direct update to Edition 5's Colorado River coverage. We told readers the December 31, 2026 deadline was the key date. The more immediate date is now late July — when the Final Environmental Impact Statement is expected to be published. That document is the legal framework that determines water allocation rules for Arizona, California, and Nevada for the next decade. Its publication triggers the formal Record of Decision process and sets the terms for every water availability certificate issued in Phoenix and Las Vegas in 2027 and beyond. USBR Post-2026 ↗

California SB 79 — New Housing Density Law Effective July 1

California's Abundant and Affordable Homes Near Transit Act took effect July 1, 2026. It overrides local height and density limits and enables streamlined ministerial approval for qualifying transit-oriented multifamily projects in LA, Orange, San Diego, Bay Area, and Sacramento counties. CA Legislature ↗

For ready-mix concrete producers, framing contractors, and MEP subcontractors operating in the five affected California county areas — this is a permit acceleration story. Projects that were previously blocked by local height restrictions or density limits can now move forward under streamlined ministerial approval, which removes discretionary review and significantly compresses permit timelines. The practical effect is that multifamily projects in transit corridors can move from planning to permit faster than at any point in California's recent history. Watch for an uptick in multifamily permit applications and groundbreakings in the Bay Area and LA Basin through Q3 and Q4 2026 as developers take advantage of the new framework.

Labor Market — 349,000 Workers Needed Nationally

The construction industry needs to attract an estimated 349,000 net new workers in 2026 to meet demand. More than 80% of firms report difficulty filling hourly craft and salaried positions — the highest level in recent years. The shortage is most acute in Arizona, California, Washington, and Nevada — exactly the four states where construction demand is growing fastest. ABC ↗ AGC ↗

The TSMC acceleration in Phoenix is compounding the Desert Southwest labor picture. Equipment installation for Fab 2 requires highly specialized cleanroom trades — electricians, pipefitters, and semiconductor construction specialists — that are being drawn from the broader western US labor pool. A contractor bidding highway work in Phoenix right now is competing for electricians against a $165 billion semiconductor investment. That competition is real and it is reflected in wage premiums. Any Q3 or Q4 project in the Desert Southwest or SoCal should lock crew commitments before going to bid — not after winning. BLS ↗

Immigration enforcement impact: California and Arizona are experiencing an estimated 12% reduction in available construction labor force since new immigration enforcement guidelines began in May 2026. This is compressing the already-tight skilled trade availability in SoCal and the Phoenix metro simultaneously. Factor this into crew assembly timelines for all Q3 and Q4 work. DOL ↗
