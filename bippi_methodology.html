How the BIPPI is Calculated

Every number in the Build Intel Price Pressure Index is traceable to a public data source. This page explains exactly how the index is built, what goes into it, and why we believe it reflects construction cost reality more accurately than CPI.

Why We Built a Separate Index

The federal government's Consumer Price Index tells you what's happening to consumer prices. It does not tell you what's happening to your cost structure as a construction producer.

CPI tracks a basket of consumer goods and services — groceries, rent, cars, healthcare, entertainment. It uses several methodological adjustments designed to account for quality improvements and behavioral changes. These adjustments make sense for measuring consumer purchasing power. They do not reflect the experience of an asphalt producer or a ready-mix concrete company in California whose two biggest inputs are petroleum-based binder and diesel fuel.

When the federal government reported 2.1% inflation for Q1-Q2 2026, an asphalt producer in Los Angeles was experiencing something closer to 6-7% input cost inflation driven by the closure of two major California refineries. That gap between reported inflation and experienced inflation is real, it matters for business planning, and it deserves its own measurement.

The Build Intel Price Pressure Index (BIPPI) is our answer to that gap. It is not a government statistic. It is a practical, transparent, sector-specific tool built for western US construction producers who need to understand what their actual cost structure is doing week to week.

How It Works — Step by Step
1

We identify the inputs that actually drive your cost structure

For each sector we identified the 4-5 inputs that represent the majority of variable production costs. For an asphalt producer, those are: asphalt oil binder, diesel fuel, aggregates, and labor. For a ready-mix concrete producer: cement, aggregates, labor, diesel, and energy overhead. We did not include inputs that are minor or fixed costs — the index is designed to track the variables that move your margins.

2

We assign weights based on typical cost composition

Each input is assigned a percentage weight that reflects its approximate share of total variable production costs for a typical western US producer. These weights are based on industry cost structures and firsthand knowledge of how materials costs actually break down in practice across western US construction projects.

Example: Asphalt oil binder is weighted at 40% in BIPPI-A because binder typically represents the single largest variable cost in asphalt mix production — often 40-50% of the total mix cost. Aggregates are weighted at 25% reflecting their share of total hot mix asphalt cost. Diesel is weighted at 10% — lower than you might expect — because asphalt plant energy consumption (natural gas and electricity) is tracked separately at 10%, and diesel primarily represents delivery and equipment fuel rather than production energy.
3

We collect current prices from public, authoritative sources

Every data point in the BIPPI comes from a named, publicly accessible source that you can verify yourself. We use the Caltrans Statewide Crude Oil Price Index for binder, the EIA for diesel, BLS for labor wages, and USGS commodity summaries for aggregates. No proprietary data, no black box inputs.

Sources used this week: Binder — Caltrans (dot.ca.gov) · Diesel — EIA (eia.gov) · Labor — BLS FRED series CES2000000003 · Aggregates — USGS MCS 2026 · Cement — USGS + Argus Media
4

We calculate the percentage change from the base period

The base period is March 2026 = 100. For each input we calculate the percentage change from the March 2026 price to the current price. Then we multiply that percentage change by the input's weight to get its contribution to the overall index. We add all contributions together to get the BIPPI reading.

5

We compare the result to CPI for the same period

Every BIPPI publication includes a direct comparison to the BLS Consumer Price Index for Urban Consumers (CPI-U) for the same time period. This gives you an immediate read on how your sector's inflation compares to the headline number. The gap between BIPPI and CPI is the most important number we publish — it tells you how far off a CPI-based budget assumption would be for your specific business.

The Formula

BIPPI Calculation Formula

BIPPI = 100 + ∑ [ ( Current Price − Base Price ) ÷ Base Price × Weight × 100 ]

Where: Base Price = price in March 2026 · Weight = component weight as a decimal (e.g. 40% = 0.40) · ∑ = sum across all components

Why BIPPI Differs From CPI — The Methodology Gap

This is not a political statement. CPI is a well-constructed index for what it is designed to measure — consumer price inflation for urban households. The problem is that it is frequently used as a proxy for all inflation, including business input cost inflation, which it was never designed to measure. Here are the specific methodological differences that cause BIPPI and CPI to diverge:

BIPPI vs. CPI — Key Methodological Differences

What BIPPI does

Tracks actual prices paid for specific construction inputs — no adjustments
Uses fixed weights that reflect real cost composition for each sector
Tracks California diesel specifically — not a national average — because our readers operate in the West
Updates weekly on key inputs (diesel, binder) not just monthly
Makes every data source publicly named and verifiable
Tracks industrial inputs relevant to construction producers

What CPI does differently

Applies hedonic quality adjustments — if a product improves, its price is adjusted downward even if you pay more
Uses substitution bias — assumes consumers switch to cheaper alternatives when prices rise
Uses Owner's Equivalent Rent instead of actual home prices — a theoretical estimate of housing costs
Tracks consumer goods and services, not industrial input costs
Uses geometric weighting which tends to produce lower readings than arithmetic weighting
Updates monthly with a lag — not responsive to weekly input price moves
Why You Can Trust These Numbers

🔍

Every source is named and linked

Every data point in BIPPI comes from a named public source with a direct URL. You can click through and verify every number yourself. No proprietary data, no black box inputs.

⚖

Government data sources only

Primary sources are BLS, EIA, USGS, and Caltrans — the same agencies that supply data to the Federal Reserve, financial markets, and government contractors. Authoritative, consistent, and non-commercial.

📋

Methodology is published and fixed

The weights and methodology are published here and will not change without a documented version update. A consistent methodology is what makes the index comparable over time.

📈

Base period is fixed and documented

March 2026 = 100. Every reading is expressed as a change from that fixed baseline. We will never retroactively change the base period without clearly documenting the change.

🛠

Built by someone inside the industry

The BIPPI was developed by Matt Pound, a western US construction industry professional with firsthand experience in the western construction market. The component weights reflect real industry cost structures verified against actual project cost data — not academic estimates.

⚠

Limitations are disclosed

We publish the limitations of the index alongside the index itself. No measurement tool is perfect — knowing where the gaps are helps you use the data appropriately.

Known Limitations

What BIPPI does not capture — disclosed transparently

1.Weights are estimates based on typical western US cost structures. Your specific company's cost structure may differ significantly — especially if you operate specialized equipment, use recycled materials, or have unusual delivery distances. BIPPI is a market-level indicator, not a company-level cost model.
2.Some input prices are estimated where monthly official data is not yet available. We use interpolation from quarterly PPI data and industry reports for cement and aggregates in months where USGS has not yet published official figures. Estimated values are noted in the data.
3.Labor data is national, not western-specific. BLS construction hourly earnings are reported at the national level. Western US wages — particularly in CA and AZ — are running higher than the national average due to immigration enforcement impacts and skilled trade shortages. BIPPI therefore understates labor cost pressure for California and Arizona producers specifically.
4.Water and energy overhead is a proxy estimate. The 5% water/energy component in BIPPI-C uses a general estimate pending better plant-level data from western US ready-mix producers. We are actively working to source better data for this component.
5.BIPPI does not capture project-level cost variation. Insurance, equipment depreciation, overhead allocation, and subcontractor markups all affect your actual project costs and are not captured in BIPPI. The index tracks input commodity costs only.
Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use BIPPI to justify price increases to my customers?

BIPPI provides documented, sourced data on input cost inflation that you can reference in customer conversations. However it is a market-level index — not a project-specific cost analysis. For contractual price adjustment clauses, you should reference the specific input prices most relevant to your contract scope rather than the composite index.

How often is BIPPI updated?

BIPPI is updated weekly alongside the main Build Intel report. Diesel and asphalt binder data update weekly from EIA and Caltrans. Labor and cement data update monthly from BLS and USGS. When monthly data is not yet available we use the most recent confirmed data point and note where estimates are used.

Why did you choose March 2026 as the base period?

March 2026 was selected as the base period because it is the earliest month for which we have reasonably complete data across all BIPPI components for 2026. It also predates the California refinery crisis peak impact, making it a meaningful starting point for measuring how costs have moved since the closures became operational.

Will you add more sector indexes?

Yes. BIPPI-A (Asphalt) and BIPPI-C (Concrete) are the first two sector indexes. Future additions include BIPPI-H (Highway/Infrastructure GC), BIPPI-V (Vertical Construction), and BIPPI-AG (Aggregates Supplier). We will add each sector when we have sufficient confidence in the component weights and data sources. Reader feedback on what sector matters most to your business helps us prioritize.

How do I suggest a correction or data improvement?

Email feedback directly through The Build Intel Substack. We take data quality seriously and will publish corrections transparently in the version log alongside the weekly report. If you have access to better western US pricing data for any component — particularly aggregates or cement — we want to hear from you.

Is BIPPI affiliated with any government agency or trade association?

No. BIPPI is an independent index published by The Build Intel. It uses publicly available government data sources (BLS, EIA, USGS, Caltrans) but is not affiliated with, endorsed by, or sponsored by any government agency, trade association, or commercial data provider.
