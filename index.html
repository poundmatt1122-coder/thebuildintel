This Week

California diesel dropped $0.23 this week to $6.784/gallon — the first meaningful relief in months. Don't get comfortable. Projections show CA fuel costs could reach $8.44/gallon by year-end as refinery inventory buffers deplete in Q3-Q4.

The short-term diesel relief is real but it masks a structural problem that is not going away. With 310,000 barrels per day of California refining capacity permanently offline — Phillips 66 Wilmington closed October 2025, Valero Benicia closed January 2026 — the state is increasingly dependent on imported fuel. As summer driving demand peaks and construction season hits full stride, that import dependence will be tested. The smart move is to treat this week's price dip as a window to add fuel escalation clauses to any open contracts, not as a signal that the problem is solved.

"A 100MW hyperscale data center requires 40,000–60,000 tons of crushed stone, 80,000–120,000 cubic yards of concrete, and 10,000–20,000 tons of steel. The western US has dozens of these in the pipeline. The aggregate supply chain was not built for this demand."

The data center materials story is becoming clearer every week. The sheer volume of aggregate and concrete required per facility is pulling supply from every other sector in the West. Clark County, Washington has only 21 years of aggregate reserves remaining. Urban markets in Phoenix, Las Vegas, and the Inland Empire are already seeing delivery lead times extend. Meanwhile the IIJA expires September 30 — 101 days from now — and Congress has not yet moved on reauthorization. If federal highway funding pauses, the stable civil/highway pipeline that has anchored western construction backlogs for four years could slow significantly heading into 2027.

Immigration enforcement — CA and AZ workforce impact TSP ↗

Increased I-9 audits in CA and AZ reducing available workforce. Contractors reporting 10–15% labor force uncertainty. Compounding existing skilled trade shortages in open-shop markets.

Takeaway: Your true labor cost is $50.93/hour — not the $41.20 BLS wage figure. Build that number into every bid. For Q3–Q4 projects in CA, AZ, or NV secure crews now with signed agreements and retention bonuses. The market is not loosening.

West Region Spotlight

Southern California Priority sub-region

Caltrans SoCal letting active weekly · Ready-mix prices elevated in SoCal · Tariff cost pressure on cement imports · Wildfire rebuilding accelerating

SoCal carries compounding pressure this week. The diesel relief this week — down to $6.784/gal from $7.018 last week — is welcome but the structural refinery problem is unchanged. Ready-mix pricing in SoCal is running $175–200/CY driven by the 25% tariff on Canadian and Mexican cement imports — a cost pressure story, not a supply shortage. Western US cement supply is adequate. The real squeeze is margin compression from input cost inflation hitting ready-mix producers. Wildfire rebuilding is beginning to accelerate in the LA Basin, adding residential and infrastructure demand on top of an already tight supply chain. Inland Empire industrial construction remains strong. San Diego military pipeline active. Data center development facing opposition in multiple jurisdictions. Any contractor working in SoCal right now is navigating the tightest cost environment in the western US.
